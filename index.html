The Reality

Every Building Project Has a Specification.
Is Your Product In It?

When a building project is planned — a county facility in Kane County, a school in Western Springs, a healthcare campus in Indiana — an architectural firm creates the construction documents. Inside those documents is a specification for every single product in that building.

Ceiling tiles. Air barriers. Cladding systems. HVAC components. Flooring. Structural products. Every one has a brand listed. Often three manufacturers per category. Those are the brands that get considered. Those are the brands that win the work.

If you're not in the specification, you don't get a shot at the sale. There's also a hard cutoff — typically two weeks before bid, the architect stops accepting addendums. After that window closes, no new brands are added. It doesn't matter how good your product is.

Most manufacturers know their product inside and out. What they don't have is someone in the room with the architect early enough, with the right technical documentation, making the case for inclusion. That's what I do.

Think of Tyvek — that white air barrier wrap you see before siding goes on. DuPont owns that spec on most projects. A competitor with a better or cheaper product has almost no chance unless someone advocates to get them listed. That's exactly the kind of work we do every day.

Not every product is a fit — and if yours isn't, I'll tell you that upfront. Every engagement starts with an honest look at whether the spec pipeline is actually there.
